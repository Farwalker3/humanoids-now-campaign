The problem

Humanoid robotics is booming, but its bodies are an afterthought. Mechanisms are designed for the demo, not for how a real body moves. There’s no shared, accurate, open foundation for the anatomy of a robot — so everyone re-guesses it, badly, behind closed doors.

What we’re building

An open platform that turns real anatomy into functional robots, in four layers:

  • Real skeletons. We take actual scanned specimen data (µCT, structured-light) and articulate true bone geometry — no sculpted approximations.
  • A working body. Hill-type muscles with real attachment points drive the bones; a nervous-system model routes commands through named nerves with reflexes; a cardiovascular–metabolic model powers and fatigues it. It has to actually stand and move, not just look right.
  • An anatomy viewer. Peel any creature layer by layer — skin, muscle, organs, skeleton — in the browser.
  • A path to hardware. The accurate skeleton is the biomimetic blueprint for a real robot chassis: 3D-printed frame, smart-servo “muscles,” an edge-AI brain, cameras for eyes, an IMU for balance.

Our open anatomy viewer — real skeletal, muscular, vascular, visceral and nervous layers, toggleable in the browser.

What already exists (today, not someday)

This isn’t a concept. We’ve built and open-sourced a real, working foundation:

  • A complete real mouse skeleton reconstructed from published µCT scan data.
  • A rabbit skeleton articulated from real museum surface scans.
  • A physiology engine: real Hill-muscle actuation, a nervous-system layer (you can sever a nerve and watch exactly its muscles go silent), and a heart/oxygen/fatigue model.
  • A browser anatomy viewer with layer-by-layer dissection.

A real mouse skeleton, reconstructed from scan data — the kind of accurate body a robot should be built on.

Our honesty pledge (and Kickstarter’s rule). Every image on this page is our own real work — renders of real scan data and real simulation output. No CGI of a finished robot that doesn’t exist yet. When we show you the physical prototype, it will be the physical prototype.

Modular by design — a testbed for prosthetics

Because our robots are built from real anatomy, their limbs are not just parts — they’re accurate, instrumented, swappable modules that share a common interface. A powered knee, ankle, or arm can be mounted on a repeatable, biomimetic body, run through real motion, and measured. That makes a lifelike robot the ideal open platform for prosthetics and neurobionics R&D: the same reason the field has rallied around shared, open hardware.

Frequently asked questions

Is this a working robot already?

No — and we won’t pretend otherwise. What exists today is the open anatomy + physiology platform: real scanned skeletons, muscle/nerve/circulation simulation, and a browser viewer. This campaign funds the first physical Humanoids Now robot. Everything we show is real work at its current stage.

What does “anatomically accurate” actually mean?

The skeleton comes from real specimen scan data (µCT / structured-light), not sculpted guesses. Muscles attach at real landmarks so they produce real leverage; nerves map to real innervation; the whole thing is validated in physics — it must balance and move.

Why open-source? Don’t you lose your edge?

An accurate anatomical foundation is infrastructure. Sharing it makes robotics more honest, inspectable, and collaborative — and our edge is execution and hardware, not secrecy.

Where does the money go?

Fabrication, actuation, brain & sensors, cloud GPU, scan data, and open release/fulfilment — full breakdown is in the campaign. It includes Kickstarter fees and a buffer.

What if you don’t hit the goal?

All-or-nothing: no one is charged and no rewards ship. We’d regroup and relaunch.

Do you use animals?

No live specimens. We use already-published, ethically-sourced scan datasets and museum surface scans under their licenses.

Risks and challenges

We’re honest about what’s hard. Hardware is unforgiving: actuators, power and fabrication introduce cost and schedule risk, and a first physical prototype will take iteration. Supply & timelines for compute and components can slip; our dates carry buffer and we’ll communicate changes openly in updates. Scope discipline: the platform can always grow — we’ll ship the first real robot before chasing breadth. Our mitigation is the same principle as the whole project: build on real, verifiable foundations, show only real progress, and keep everything open so backers can check our work at every step.
